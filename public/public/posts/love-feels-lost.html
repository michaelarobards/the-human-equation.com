When Love Feels Lost

Insights from couples therapy when resentment blocks connection

It’s 3 a.m. and you’re both lying awake in the dark, inches apart yet feeling miles away. You can still remember the early days when conversation flowed and laughter came easily, but lately the air between you is thick with unspoken grievances and tiredness. When couples tell me they’ve “fallen out of love,” what they’re really saying is this: the hurt and the withdrawals have accumulated to the point that they can no longer access the love that brought them together.

Over time, small betrayals and chronic stress become layers of scar tissue around the heart. Resentment is not a sudden event; it’s a slow building of unanswered bids for connection. Each dismissed feeling, every unresolved argument, reinforces the story that your partner isn’t safe. Eventually, you start seeing them as an adversary rather than an ally.

“Most couples don’t fall out of love; they get locked out of it behind walls of resentment and unmet needs.”

The first step in reclaiming love is differentiation — the ability to stay connected to yourself while staying emotionally close to another. Differentiation lets you listen to your partner’s pain without drowning in guilt or defensiveness. It allows you to express your needs without blame or manipulation. Without differentiation, the relationship oscillates between enmeshment and withdrawal.

In our sessions, we slow down the conversation and track what’s happening beneath the words. When partners are triggered, their nervous systems go into survival mode: heart rate increases, breathing gets shallow, muscles tense. Learning to recognize these cues and regulate them — sometimes by simply pausing and placing a hand over your heart — creates space for empathy and curiosity to return.

If love feels lost in your relationship, know that it often isn’t gone — it’s buried under layers of pain, fear and protective strategies. Couples therapy and relationship groups offer a structured, compassionate place to practice differentiation, repair resentments and reawaken what’s been there all along. The work is uncomfortable, but discovering a deeper, more resilient love on the other side is worth every step.
