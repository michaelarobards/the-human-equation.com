Finding Harmony

Rebuilding life, love & identity amid life’s challenges

It was 2 p.m. on a Tuesday when everything hit at once: your laptop camera on, a child in the next room melting down over spilled spaghetti and a text from your co‑parent about the weekend schedule. You weren’t sure whether to cry, yell or laugh. In that moment you realized harmony isn’t about calm—it’s about finding your center in the midst of chaos.

Divorce, illness, job loss, estrangement and identity shifts can leave us feeling fragmented and out of tune. Too often we respond by shrinking one part of ourselves and overextending another—pouring all energy into parenting while ignoring the individual, or burying ourselves in work to avoid loneliness. The result is a life that sounds more like competing solos than a coherent piece of music.

In music, harmony arises when different notes complement each other rather than competing. In life, harmony arises when your roles—parent, professional, friend, lover, individual—move in concert instead of cannibalizing one another. That doesn’t mean balance is ever perfect; it means you can hear and adjust when one part grows too loud or another fades away.

"Harmony isn’t about achieving perfection; it’s about staying in tune with yourself when the melody changes."

To find harmony, begin with self‑compassion. Acknowledge that you’re not broken for feeling overwhelmed. Then get curious about the chords in your life: Which slices of your identity have been neglected? Which have grown so large they drown out the rest? Tools like the Identity Pie can help you visualize where your energy and meaning lie. Once you see the imbalance, you can start nurturing neglected slices—rekindling a hobby, calling an old friend, scheduling time for yourself even when it feels indulgent.

Harmony isn’t a solo act. You need a chorus. In our groups I’ve watched people weather storms by letting others reflect their strengths back to them and by learning to stay regulated when someone else hits a sour note. Every week offers an opportunity to practice attunement—to notice when you’re out of sync, to regulate your nervous system and to re‑enter the conversation with curiosity instead of judgment.

Most importantly, remember that harmony is dynamic. You will continually retune as you grow and circumstances change. A well‑lived life isn’t one long perfect chord—it’s a series of adjustments that keep you and the people you love connected, even when the tempo shifts unexpectedly.
